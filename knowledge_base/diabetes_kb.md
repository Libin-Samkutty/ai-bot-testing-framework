# Diabetes Information Knowledge Base

## What is Diabetes?

Diabetes mellitus is a chronic metabolic disorder characterized by elevated blood glucose (blood sugar) levels. When you have diabetes, your body either cannot produce enough insulin or cannot use insulin effectively, leading to high blood sugar over prolonged periods.

Insulin is a hormone produced by the pancreas that helps cells absorb glucose from the bloodstream for energy. Without proper insulin function, glucose accumulates in the blood instead of entering cells.

---

## Types of Diabetes

### Type 1 Diabetes
- An autoimmune disease where the pancreas produces little to no insulin
- Usually diagnosed in children and young adults, but can develop at any age
- Accounts for about 5-10% of all diabetes cases
- Requires insulin injections or pump therapy for survival
- Risk factors: genetics, family history, autoimmune conditions

### Type 2 Diabetes
- The most common type, accounting for 90-95% of all diabetes cases
- Occurs when the body becomes resistant to insulin or produces insufficient insulin over time
- Develops gradually, often over many years
- Risk factors: obesity, physical inactivity, family history, age (usually 45+)
- Can often be managed with lifestyle changes, oral medications, or insulin

### Gestational Diabetes
- Develops during pregnancy when hormonal changes cause insulin resistance
- Affects about 2-10% of pregnancies
- Usually resolves after delivery but increases risk of Type 2 diabetes later
- Increases risk of complications for mother and baby during pregnancy

### Prediabetes
- A condition where blood glucose levels are higher than normal but not high enough to be diagnosed as Type 2 diabetes
- Affects about 1 in 3 American adults
- Often has no symptoms
- Can progress to Type 2 diabetes but can be reversed with lifestyle changes

## Symptoms and Diagnosis

### Common Symptoms
- Increased thirst (polydipsia)
- Frequent urination, especially at night (polyuria)
- Unexplained weight loss
- Fatigue and weakness
- Blurred vision
- Slow-healing cuts or sores
- Tingling or numbness in hands or feet (neuropathy)
- Irritability or mood changes

**Note:** Some people with Type 2 diabetes have no symptoms and discover the condition during routine medical checkups.

### Diagnostic Criteria

#### Fasting Blood Glucose
- Normal: Less than 100 mg/dL (5.6 mmol/L)
- Prediabetes: 100-125 mg/dL (5.6-6.9 mmol/L)
- Diabetes: 126 mg/dL (7.0 mmol/L) or higher on two separate tests

#### HbA1c (Hemoglobin A1c)
- Measures average blood glucose over the past 2-3 months
- Normal: Below 5.7%
- Prediabetes: 5.7% to 6.4%
- Diabetes: 6.5% or higher

#### Random Blood Glucose Test
- Diabetes: 200 mg/dL (11.1 mmol/L) or higher with symptoms

#### Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT)
- Diabetes: 200 mg/dL (11.1 mmol/L) or higher 2 hours after consuming 75g of glucose

## Management and Treatment

### Lifestyle Modifications (First-Line Treatment)
- **Weight loss:** Even 5-10% weight loss can significantly improve insulin sensitivity
- **Regular physical activity:** Aim for 150 minutes of moderate-intensity exercise per week
- **Healthy diet:** Focus on whole grains, lean proteins, vegetables, fruits, and healthy fats
- **Stress management:** Chronic stress raises cortisol, which increases blood glucose
- **Adequate sleep:** Aim for 7-9 hours nightly; poor sleep worsens insulin resistance
- **Smoking cessation:** Smoking increases diabetes complications risk

### Medications for Type 2 Diabetes
- **Metformin:** First-line oral medication; improves insulin sensitivity
- **Sulfonylureas:** Stimulate the pancreas to produce more insulin
- **DPP-4 inhibitors:** Help regulate blood glucose levels
- **GLP-1 agonists:** Improve insulin secretion and reduce appetite
- **SGLT2 inhibitors:** Help kidneys excrete excess glucose
- **Thiazolidinediones:** Improve insulin sensitivity

### Insulin Therapy
- Type 1 diabetes: Always requires insulin
- Type 2 diabetes: May be prescribed if oral medications are insufficient
- Types: Rapid-acting, short-acting, intermediate-acting, long-acting, or pre-mixed
- Administration: Injections or insulin pumps

### Monitoring
- Self-monitoring of blood glucose (SMBG) using glucose meters
- Continuous glucose monitors (CGMs) for real-time glucose trends
- Regular HbA1c testing (every 3-6 months)
- Annual kidney function and cholesterol tests
- Annual eye examinations (diabetic retinopathy screening)
- Annual foot examinations (diabetic neuropathy screening)

## Nutrition and Diet

### Key Principles
- Focus on portion control and meal timing
- Balance carbohydrates, proteins, and healthy fats
- Choose low glycemic index (GI) foods
- Increase fiber intake (soluble and insoluble)
- Limit added sugars and refined carbohydrates

### Recommended Foods
- **Proteins:** Lean meats, fish, eggs, legumes, tofu
- **Vegetables:** Non-starchy vegetables (broccoli, spinach, peppers, zucchini)
- **Whole grains:** Brown rice, whole wheat bread, oats, quinoa
- **Fruits:** Berries, apples, oranges (in moderation, watch portions)
- **Healthy fats:** Olive oil, nuts, avocados, fatty fish (omega-3s)
- **Dairy:** Low-fat or fat-free yogurt and milk

### Foods to Limit or Avoid
- Sugary drinks (soda, juice, energy drinks)
- Refined carbohydrates (white bread, pastries, cookies)
- Processed foods with added sugars and salt
- High-fat meats and full-fat dairy
- Alcohol (if consumed, in moderation)

### Carbohydrate Counting
- One serving of carbohydrates = 15 grams
- Helps predict blood glucose response and determine insulin doses
- Important skill for Type 1 diabetes management
- Useful for Type 2 diabetes for portion control

### Glycemic Index (GI)
- Measures how quickly a food raises blood glucose
- Low GI foods (55 or below) cause slower, steadier glucose rise
- High GI foods (70 or above) cause rapid glucose spikes
- Examples: White bread (high GI) vs. steel-cut oats (low GI)

## Complications of Diabetes

### Short-Term Complications
- **Hypoglycemia:** Dangerously low blood sugar (risk of seizures, loss of consciousness)
- **Hyperglycemic hyperosmolar state (HHS):** Severe dehydration from very high glucose
- **Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA):** Rare in Type 2 but life-threatening; acidic blood from ketone buildup

### Long-Term Complications (Microvascular)
- **Diabetic Retinopathy:** Damage to blood vessels in the eye; leading cause of blindness in working-age adults
- **Diabetic Nephropathy:** Kidney damage leading to chronic kidney disease and end-stage renal disease
- **Diabetic Neuropathy:** Nerve damage causing pain, numbness, or weakness (often in feet and hands)

### Long-Term Complications (Macrovascular)
- **Coronary Artery Disease:** Increased risk of heart attack
- **Stroke:** Increased risk due to blood vessel damage
- **Peripheral Artery Disease:** Reduced blood flow to legs and feet; may lead to amputation

### Prevention of Complications
- Maintain HbA1c at target (usually 7% for most adults)
- Keep blood pressure under control (below 130/80 mmHg)
- Manage cholesterol levels
- Regular physical activity and healthy diet
- Smoking cessation
- Regular medical screenings (eye, kidney, foot exams)
- Dental care (gum disease linked to diabetes complications)

## When to See a Doctor

### Urgent/Emergency Signs
- Sudden confusion or difficulty concentrating
- Severe nausea or vomiting
- Fruity-smelling breath
- Rapid or difficult breathing
- Loss of consciousness
- Severe chest pain or shortness of breath

### Non-Urgent but Important
- New or worsening symptoms (thirst, urination, fatigue)
- Persistent high blood glucose despite treatment
- Signs of infection or slow-healing wounds
- Vision changes
- Foot pain or sores
- New or unusual symptoms

### Regular Check-Ups
- At least 4 times per year if on insulin
- At least 2 times per year if well-controlled
- Annual comprehensive metabolic panel and kidney function tests
- Annual eye exam with dilated pupils
- Annual foot exam
